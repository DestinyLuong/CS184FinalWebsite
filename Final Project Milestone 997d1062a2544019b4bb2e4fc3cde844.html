Authors: Aishik, Tanush, Joshua, Destiny

Project Summary

Our project aims to accurately render and physically simulate the look and behavior thin-film materials (in particular bubbles). We also hope to simulate their interactions with one another or with other objects. We’ve split our work into 4 main parts:

  1. Bubble mesh creation and physical simulation
  1. Accurate bubble (thin-film) rendering
  1. Merging and mesh changes to support multi-bubble interaction
  1. Animation, by compilation of the completely rendered images at each timestep.

Progress

This week we worked on two of the three main parts. Firstly, we focused on creating a mesh cube representing a misshapen bubble. Taking this newly created “bubble”, we use the points as inputs and begin to perturb the bubble, pushing points in when the bubble is concave, or out when the bubble is convex to create a sphere. To maintain numerical stability within the algorithm, regularly remeshing to remove degenerate geometry is required. These are our results of the process at different timesteps:

The second thing we worked on was creating a transparent, film-like texture on the bubble. At the moment, we are rendering our bubble based on a closed-form solution to the Airy summation with angle-dependent Fresnel coefficients and accounting for interference effects. We sample the spectral reflectance distribution at 3 wavelengths, with constant thickness, to determine the RGB values for its BSDF. We plan to render this image with a similar method at every time step, creating an animation where the bubble is reorganizing its mesh. Another potential update we are considering is using a spectral integration along all the wavelengths corresponding to the RGB values to give a more accurate look.

Preliminary Results

Our bubble render, at the moment, is using a very rudimentary approach to spectral integration and thickness modeling. As such, we are planning to reform our algorithm and believe our final result will be a lot closer to the results in the article Thin Film Interference for Computer Graphics as well as the paper “A Practical Extension to Microfacet Theory for the Modeling of Varying Iridescence“ [Balcour and Barla 2017].

Our goal for the project is to create a simulation of two bubbles colliding with each other and then merging. We currently have the simulation for a single bubble completed, however, we are still working on how to merge two different bubbles. Our goal is to create something similar to the article Animation of Soap Bubble Dynamics, Cluster Formation, and Collision, which details bubble physics when interacting with other objects including another bubble. The one issue with this article is that it uses a spring-based system rather than a mesh-based one, which is what we currently have modeled, however, we believe we can use some of these ideas in order to achieve a similar product.

Reflections

Currently, we have created a general bubble simulation, where a cubed bubble slowly becomes a sphere. In order to create a two-bubble simulation, we would have to change our model to instead start with two spherical bubbles which then float around (via coupling to some external force, e.g. wind / gravity) and merge. This would mean we would have to add more edge cases to the remesher to account for bubble merging. This is a very nontrivial problem, and though there are existing packages which handle it, they are poorly documented and difficult to use.

For improvements, we want to try and create a faster re-meshing and rendering system as both of these processes took quite a bit of time in our tests. As such, our goal for next week will be working on creating optimizations for these two sections before we dive into creating bubble interactions.

Furthermore, although our current bubble is transparent and colorful it is not very realistic, we plan to recreate our current BSDF model with one that has higher accuracy when it comes to conversion between its spectral reflectance and an RGB representation.
